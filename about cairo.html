HISTORY

Helsinki was established as a trading town by King Gustav I of Sweden in 1550 as the town of Helsingfors, which he intended to be a rival to the Hanseatic city of Reval (today known as Tallinn). In order to populate his newly founded town, the King issued an order to resettle the bourgeoisie of Porvoo, Ekenäs, Rauma and Ulvila into the town. Little came of the plans as Helsinki remained a tiny town plagued by poverty, wars, and diseases.

The plague of 1710 killed the greater part of the inhabitants of Helsinki. The construction of the naval fortress Sveaborg (in Finnish Viapori, today also Suomenlinna) in the 18th century helped improve Helsinki's status, but it was not until Russia defeated Sweden in the Finnish War and annexed Finland as the autonomous Grand Duchy of Finland in 1809 that the town began to develop into a substantial city. Russians besieged the Sveaborg fortress during the war, and about one quarter of the town was destroyed in an 1808 fire.

Russian Emperor Alexander I of Russia moved the Finnish capital from Turku to Helsinki in 1812[30] to reduce Swedish influence in Finland, and to bring the capital closer to Saint Petersburg. Following the Great Fire of Turku in 1827, the Royal Academy of Turku, which at the time was the country's only university, was also relocated to Helsinki and eventually became the modern University of Helsinki. The move consolidated the city's new role and helped set it on a path of continuous growth. This transformation is highly apparent in the downtown core, which was rebuilt in the neoclassical style to resemble Saint Petersburg, mostly to a plan by the German-born architect C. L. Engel. As elsewhere, technological advancements such as railroads and industrialization were key factors behind the city's growth.

Despite the tumultuous nature of Finnish history during the first half of the 20th century (including the Finnish Civil War and the Winter War which both left marks on the city), Helsinki continued its steady development. A landmark event was the 1952 Olympic Games, held in Helsinki. Finland's rapid urbanization in the 1970s, occurring late relative to the rest of Europe, tripled the population in the metropolitan area, and the Helsinki Metro subway system was built. The relatively sparse population density of Helsinki and its peculiar structure have often been attributed to the lateness of its growth.

Facts About Helsinki

1.Helsinki serves as the capital city for the Republic of Finland. Located in Europe’s northeast between Sweden, Norway and Russia’s northwestern coast, Finland is the continent’s eighth-biggest country in terms of geographic area.

Finland is the European Union’s most sparsely populated member, while Helsinki is the northernmost capital of any EU country.

Nicknames for Helsinki include “White City of the North”, “Daughter of the Baltic” or simply “Hesa” or “Stadi”.

Research website numbeo ranks Helsinki in the top 10% compared to the 250 cities evaluated on its overall quality of life index at June 2020. Helsinki scored well on index factors such as pollution (cleanest number 1), safety (top 8%), health care (upper 16%) and traffic commute time (fastest 35%).

Helsinki is noted for its superior tap water quality. The city’s water supply is provided by the 75-mile (120-kilometer) Päijänne Water Tunnel, one of the world’s longest uninterrupted rock tunnels.

Helsinki was recognized by the Guinness World Records as the site where Finland’s Marigold IceUnity team won 15 medals at the ISU World Synchronized Skating Championships from 2000 to 2019, the most ever.

Emblematic of Finland’s strong pulp and paper industry, Helsinki is also where Finn Vesa Kurki set another Guinness World Record for chain sawing 15 slices in a pre-set size from a log within 3 minutes.

Over 80% of Finland’s largest companies have their home offices in Helsinki or its surrounding metropolitan area. Top Helsinki-based businesses include financial services leader Sampo Group, pulp and paper giant UPM and international engineering firm Kone .
